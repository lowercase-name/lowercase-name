i have a fancy bike trainer. i'd love to tell you it's because i'm a real cyclist, unlike all those fakers with their tacky expensive pelotons, i'm proud of my real bike and also my, uh, high rank in zwift?

zwift is the app i'm thinking of, right

...

yeah it is, so i will tell you that, and i will make extra-sure to pretend that it's not due to peloton's used bike fee making the trainer much cheaper in practice. i will also tell you that i totally have it to make me healthy, even though i don't really bother to track my exercise, or diet, or really do anything other than pedal when i feel like it and get excited when the computer says i went full speed for an hour, because "cardio for the sake of it is fun" is a statement of the deranged.

and, of course, i'm not deranged at all. nothing to see here, officer.

but i also have pretty bad legs, a consequence of one of those chicken-or-egg hypotheticals turned reality, where you have a laundry list of interconnected diseases, and you know one of them definitely caused the others, but you're in so deep you'll never know which. lymphedema, CVI, POTS, and as of right now, some unspecified probably-EDS-maybe connective tissue thing going on, a lot of issues that, tacked on with legs so long they make up some 60-70% of my body mass, means i'm kind of messed up in the leg department of things. i can get some very expensive compression garments you have to import from germany, and they help with some of it, but not nearly all of it.
*[CVI]: chronic venous insufficiency
*[POTS]: postural orthostatic tachycardia syndrome
*[EDS]: ehlers–danlos syndrome

if you asked the median person online, these sound like two wildly antithetical statements. they imagine the hypothetical disabled person, and they start imagining what is someone totally unable to do anything related to their condition, a cursed description that, while not being quite right, is also not technically *wrong*. i do, in fact, need the priority seats on a train, because if it wobbles even a little bit while i'm standing, i can and will create a domino effect of injuries all around me. i cannot be trusted to stand for too long in general, really, for a few hours with my socks on or for a few *minutes* with them off.

but the "*cannot*" is always among the most tempting things to try, isn't it? i think, as humans, we have an innate desire to take things we'd fail to do, and then work our way to hitting the bare minimum necessary to get there. it's how we advance as society, our need to turn *not enough* into *just barely enough* being the reason i can type on a computer, from one side of the world, and you can read it from the opposite.

i think, when you're disabled --- and if you live for long enough, that is a *when* --- that desire will find a way to sneak up on you. i don't recall liking cardio before any of this went south, but i do recall, not too long after needing a mobility cane to get around, suddenly yearning to walk in increments of kilometers, not meters.

there's ways to appease that beast in all of us, some healthier than others. i'd guess that a *try to stand in a crowded bus as long as possible and also don't hold onto anything* challenge would be fairly unhealthy, if not for me, then for the sternums of everyone i'd crash into. i think, if that bike were an actual bike i rode on the street, it would perhaps be dangerous... but in my home, attached to a trainer so solid that it can't even budge a little, what risk would i be taking? my legs might hurt? they already do, what's another .5% when the pain is already 300% of what i should be able to tolerate?

it's not a substitute for treatment, and i'm not so deranged as to think i could do this without the fabric covering my lower body that costed eight hundred real life dollars (jesus christ), but i think, for the soul, it's healthier to be willing to test how you feel. if nothing else, i can very confidently say that even while standing on some of the worst legs known to modern living, i can absolutely, without a shadow of a doubt, out-cardio many of the able-bodied who can't be bothered.
